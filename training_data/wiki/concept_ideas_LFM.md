# LiquidAI LFM:

## Curriculum Expansion for Elementary Conversational AI

To bridge the gap between a "dictionary of objects" and a "conversational peer," the model needs to understand the glue that binds these objects together: human intent, social structures, and physical processes.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Family and Social Roles**
**EXAMPLES:** Mother, father, sister, brother, friend, teacher**

**DEPENDS ON:** Home, farm, school (implied), feeling

**SEQUENCE:** Early

**REASON:** Children define their world through their primary relationships and the people they interact with daily.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Daily Routines and Habits**
**EXAMPLES:** Brushing teeth, getting dressed, breakfast, nap, playtime, chores**

**DEPENDS ON:** Tooth, shirt, pants, morning, afternoon, evening, bed

**SEQUENCE:** Early

**REASON:** Routine provides the chronological framework for almost all elementary-level storytelling and conversation.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Communication Actions**
**EXAMPLES:** Asking, telling, listening, whispering, shouting, writing**

**DEPENDS ON:** Mouth, ear, paper, crayon, book, truth, fact

**SEQUENCE:** Early

**REASON:** The model needs to understand the mechanics of the conversation it is currently having.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Advanced Emotions and Empathy**
**EXAMPLES:** Scared, angry, excited, lonely, proud, surprised**

**DEPENDS ON:** Happy, sad, feeling, face, heart (implied)

**SEQUENCE:** Middle

**REASON:** Moving beyond basic biological states allows the model to respond appropriately to a child’s social anecdotes.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Health and Wellness**
**EXAMPLES:** Sickness, hurt, medicine, rest, germs, healthy**

**DEPENDS ON:** Body (all parts), skin, bone, soup, water, wash (implied)

**SEQUENCE:** Middle

**REASON:** Kids frequently talk about minor injuries or why they stayed home from school, requiring a "system" view of the body.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Movement and Physical Action**
**EXAMPLES:** Running, jumping, climbing, throwing, catching, falling**

**DEPENDS ON:** Leg, arm, hand, ball, hill, tree, ground (implied)

**SEQUENCE:** Early

**REASON:** Elementary-age life is highly kinetic; describing play requires verbs that connect the body to objects.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Natural Life Cycles**
**EXAMPLES:** Growing, blooming, hatching, aging, dying, seasons**

**DEPENDS ON:** Seed (implied), tree, flower, egg, bird, butterfly, time

**SEQUENCE:** Middle

**REASON:** This connects static objects (like "seed" and "tree") into a logical, temporal narrative of change.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Measurement and Comparison**
**EXAMPLES:** Heavier/lighter, taller/shorter, faster/slower, more/less**

**DEPENDS ON:** Size, weight, speed, number, all/some/none

**SEQUENCE:** Middle

**REASON:** Children constantly categorize their world by comparing themselves to others or objects to each other.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Community and Professions**
**EXAMPLES:** Doctor, police officer, farmer, pilot, baker, mail carrier**

**DEPENDS ON:** Car, plane, farm, bread, coat, help (implied)

**SEQUENCE:** Late

**REASON:** Expands the child’s world view from the immediate "home/school" bubble to the broader functioning society.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Ownership and Sharing**
**EXAMPLES:** Mine, yours, giving, taking, gift, belonging**

**DEPENDS ON:** Objects, doll, ball, book, home, friend

**SEQUENCE:** Early

**REASON:** Much of early social negotiation revolves around the concept of property and the ethics of sharing.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Weather Processes and Effects**
**EXAMPLES:** Melting, freezing, drying, soaking, blowing**

**DEPENDS ON:** Ice, sun, rain, snow, wind, water, heat/cold (implied)

**SEQUENCE:** Middle

**REASON:** It explains the "why" behind the natural world concepts the model already knows (e.g., why snow becomes a puddle).

---

### **CATEGORY: **School and Learning**
**EXAMPLES:** Lesson, recess, desk, backpack, homework, grade**

**DEPENDS ON:** Desk, book, paper, crayon, chair, teacher, friend

**SEQUENCE:** Early

**REASON:** For a 6–10 year old, school is the primary "workplace" and a major source of conversational topics.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Social Manners and Rules**
**EXAMPLES:** Please/thank you, waiting, turn-taking, fair, mistake, sorry**

**DEPENDS ON:** Logic (rule), goal, problem, solution, friend

**SEQUENCE:** Early

**REASON:** To be an "authentic" peer, the model must operate within the same social constraints as the child.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Construction and Material Change**
**EXAMPLES:** Breaking, fixing, building, tearing, folding, gluing**

**DEPENDS ON:** Hammer, screw, brick, wood, paper, stick, problem

**SEQUENCE:** Middle

**REASON:** Children are active makers; they need to describe the transition of materials from one state to another.

---

### **CATEGORY: **Sensory Experiences**
**EXAMPLES:** Loud, quiet, soft, hard, sticky, stinky, sweet, sour**

**DEPENDS ON:** Ear, skin, nose, mouth, honey, stone, bread

**SEQUENCE:** Early

**REASON:** Adjectives describing sensory input are essential for the descriptive language used in elementary storytelling.
